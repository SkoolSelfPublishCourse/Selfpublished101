Concept Creation

Turn your idea into a writing-ready concept. Define your book, your reader, and your promise — before you write a single word.

Module 2 · Lesson Video
Overview

What You'll Accomplish

Every published book began as a single fragile idea. That idea became a concept. That concept became a plan. That plan became a book.

This module gives you the framework to transform your spark into a defined, writing-ready concept — complete with a clear genre, a specific reader, a unique angle, and a central promise. Without this foundation, manuscripts drift, stall, and contradict themselves. With it, you always know where you're going.

Module 2 Deliverable

A complete book concept: your core subject or story in 1–2 sentences, your target reader defined specifically, your genre and structure chosen, and your central promise written clearly.

Lesson Content

What You'll Learn

What Is a Book Concept?

A concept answers five essential questions: What is your book about? Who is it for? Why does it need to exist? How will it be delivered? So What — what changes for the reader?

An Idea (Starting Point)

  • "I want to write about my grandmother"
  • "A detective who solves crimes using music"
  • "A book about getting over fear"
  • "Something set in space with AI"

A Concept (Writing-Ready)

  • A memoir about immigration exploring identity across three generations
  • A noir thriller where a blind pianist solves murder through sound memories
  • A practical guide for high-achievers who use perfectionism to hide fear
  • A sci-fi novel exploring what makes us human when AI can replicate everything except regret
The Six Elements of a Strong Concept
1

Core Subject or Story

The central focus of the book, stated in 1–2 sentences without ambiguity.

2

Target Reader

A specific, identifiable person. Not "everyone" or "adults." The more specific, the stronger.

3

Genre and Format

The category your book lives in and how it will be structured.

4

Unique Angle

What makes your approach, perspective, or story different from others in your genre.

5

Central Promise

The transformation, experience, or understanding the reader will gain by finishing.

6

Emotional Core

The feeling the book will leave readers with. This is what makes books memorable.

The Quick Concept Test

Can you describe this book in two sentences to a stranger and have them immediately understand what it is and whether they want to read it? If yes — your concept is ready. If not — keep refining.

Assignments

Your Work

Complete each assignment before moving to the next module.

2.1

Your Core Concept Statement

Write your book concept in 2 sentences. Sentence 1: What it is and who it's for. Sentence 2: What the reader gains and what makes it unique.

2.2

Your Target Reader Profile

Describe your ideal reader in detail: age, life stage, what they want, what they fear, what they already know, and where they find books.

2.3

Genre, Structure & Comp Titles

State your primary genre, sub-genre, structure model, and 2–3 recently published comp titles that share your audience.

2.4

Your Opening Paragraph (Draft)

Write the first paragraph of your book. This will change — but commit to something now. It must create immediate curiosity, tension, or emotional connection.
