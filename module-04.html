What You'll Learn

The One Rule of Module 4

Do not edit while you draft. Writing and editing are different cognitive modes. Editing while drafting is the single most common reason manuscripts never get finished. Write forward. Fix it in Module 5.

Lesson 1: The First Draft Mindset

A first draft is not a finished book. It is a thinking tool — a way of discovering what you actually want to say by saying it badly first. Every professional author knows this. The difference between writers who finish and writers who don't: finishers give themselves permission to write badly.

What a First Draft Is For

  • To discover what your book actually wants to be
  • To get your ideas out of your head and onto the page
  • To create raw material you can shape in revision
  • To prove to yourself that you can finish

What a First Draft Is Not

  • Not a polished product ready for readers
  • Not a representation of your writing ability
  • Not something anyone else needs to see
  • Not something you should judge while writing it
Lesson 2: Your Daily Writing System

Consistency beats intensity. Writing 500 words every day for 30 days produces 15,000 words. Writing 1,000 words daily produces a full-length manuscript.

1

Same time, same place, every day

Routine trains your brain to write on demand.

2

Reread the last paragraph before starting

This reconnects you to your voice and momentum.

3

Write your chapter assignment for the day

Use the 30-day plan below as your daily prompt.

4

When you hit your word count, stop

Even mid-sentence if needed. This creates forward pull into tomorrow's session.

5

Log your word count in the tracker

This creates accountability and momentum.

Lesson 3: The Three Chapter Questions

Before writing any chapter, answer these three questions:

1

What does this chapter do?

Its one job — from your outline.

2

How does this chapter begin?

The hook — an image, question, or moment that pulls the reader in.

3

How does this chapter end?

The handoff — what makes the reader want to keep going?

Your 30-Day Writing Plan

Write your chapters in a separate document (your manuscript file). Return here to log your word count and mark each day complete. The prompts below guide you into each chapter — adapt them to your specific book and outline.

Days 1–7

Week 1 · Building the Foundation

Ch.1: Your opening — strongest hook, establish voice. Ch.2: Setup — world, problem, context. Ch.3: Inciting event or core argument. Ch.4: Deepening — character, relationships, evidence. Ch.5: First obstacle or complication. Ch.6: Response and adaptation. Ch.7: The Turn — something shifts.

Days 8–14

Week 2 · Rising Tension

Ch.8: Raise the stakes. Ch.9: New information. Ch.10: The Midpoint — something significant happens. Ch.11: Fallout from the midpoint. Ch.12: Pressure builds. Ch.13: The unexpected. Ch.14: Commitment — doubling down.

Days 15–21

Week 3 · The Turn

Ch.15: Dark moment approaches. Ch.16: Everything falls apart — the lowest point. Ch.17: The turning point — things begin to turn. Ch.18: Building toward resolution. Ch.19: The climax — write it with everything you have. Ch.20: Resolution begins. Ch.21: Deepening the resolution.

Days 22–30

Week 4 · The Finish

Ch.22: Address what remains. Ch.23: The new normal. Ch.24: Looking back. Ch.25: Looking forward. Days 26–29: Remaining chapters from your outline. Day 30: The final page. Write "END OF FIRST DRAFT" and today's date.
