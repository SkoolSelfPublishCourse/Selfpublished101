Module 03 · Phase One · Writing

Book Architecture

Build your complete chapter-by-chapter outline before you write a word. By the end of this module, your book exists — it just hasn't been written yet.

Module 3 · Lesson Video
Overview

What You'll Accomplish

You have your genre. You have your concept. Now it is time to build the structure that will hold your book together from first page to last. This module is about architecture — not writing prose, but designing the skeleton that your words will eventually fill.

Writers who skip this step write themselves into corners. They stall at chapter five because they don't know where the story is going. A solid outline prevents all of this.

Module 3 Deliverable

A complete chapter-by-chapter outline — every chapter named, described in one sentence, and placed in the right order.

Lesson Content

What You'll Learn

Lesson 1: The Three Pillars of Book Architecture
I

The Promise

Every chapter must either fulfill your book's promise or build toward fulfilling it. Ask about every chapter: does this move the reader closer to the promise I made them? If no — remove it or rework it.

II

The Arc

Your book has a beginning, a middle, and an end. The arc is the shape of your book's journey — where it starts emotionally or intellectually, how it builds, and where it arrives.

III

The Chapters

Each chapter has a job: to do one thing clearly and well, then hand the reader off to the next with enough momentum to keep going. Every chapter needs one clear purpose, one clear hook, and one clear reason to keep reading.

Fiction Arc

  • Opening: establish character, world, and desire
  • Rising tension: obstacles and complications
  • Midpoint: stakes become undeniable
  • Dark night: everything seems lost
  • Climax: central conflict resolves
  • Resolution: the new normal

Non-Fiction Arc

  • Opening: establish the problem and reader pain
  • Foundation: the knowledge they need
  • Framework: your system or argument
  • Application: how it works in practice
  • Deepening: nuance and complexity
  • Transformation: the reader is changed
Lesson 2: How to Build Your Chapter Outline
1

Brain Dump

Write down everything you know needs to be in your book. Every scene, argument, story, idea. Don't organize yet — just get it all out. Aim for 30–60 items.

2

Cluster

Group related items. Look for natural clusters of 3–5 items that belong together. Each cluster will likely become a chapter.

3

Order

Arrange clusters in the order that best serves your structure model. Which cluster must come before which for the reader to understand what comes next?

4

Name and Describe

Give each cluster a working chapter title and one sentence describing what it does. If you can't write it in one sentence — the chapter doesn't have a clear enough focus yet.

5

Check the Arc

Read your chapter list from beginning to end. Does it follow your chosen arc? Does each chapter build on the last? Does the ending deliver on the opening?

The Outline Test

Read just the one-sentence descriptions of your chapters in order. If someone who hasn't read your concept can follow the shape of the book — understand what it's about, feel the build, and sense where it's going — your outline is ready.

Assignments

Your Work

Complete each assignment before moving to the next module.

3.1

Your Book's Arc

In 3–4 sentences, describe the arc of your book: where it begins emotionally or intellectually, what happens in the middle that builds or complicates, and where it lands at the end.

3.2

Brain Dump

Write down every single thing you know needs to be in your book. Don't edit or organize. Just list. Aim for at least 20 items.

3.3

Your Chapter Map

Organize your brain dump into chapters. Give each chapter a working title and one-sentence purpose. Aim for 15–25 chapters.

Your Opening Hook
