Module 05 · Phase One · Writing

Editing & Revision

Turn your first draft into a publishable manuscript. Three professional revision passes — structural, chapter-level, and line-level.

Module 5 · Lesson Video
Overview

What You'll Accomplish

A first draft is raw material. Revision is where you shape it into something worth publishing. Most writers underestimate how transformative the revision process is — and how much of the real writing happens here, not in the first draft.

This module walks you through three levels of revision. By the time you complete all three passes, you will have a manuscript that is coherent, focused, and compelling.

Module 5 Deliverable

A revised, polished manuscript ready for final proofreading and the publishing process in Phase Two.

Lesson Content

What You'll Learn

Before You Begin

Put your manuscript away for at least 2–3 days before starting revision. Distance gives you perspective. You need to be able to read your draft as a reader, not as the person who wrote it.

The Three-Pass System

The Three Passes

  • Pass 1: Structural Revision
  • Pass 2: Chapter-Level Revision
  • Pass 3: Line-Level Revision

What Each Fixes

  • The overall architecture — does the book work as a whole?
  • Each chapter — does every section do its job?
  • Every sentence — is it clear, necessary, and right?
Pass 1: Structural Revision

Read your entire manuscript in one or two sittings without stopping to make changes. Take notes only. You are looking at the architecture of the whole book.

Structural Questions to Answer

Does the opening establish the book's promise? Does each chapter do its job? Are chapters in the right order? Is anything missing? Is anything that doesn't belong? Does the ending deliver on the opening?

Pass 2: Chapter-Level Revision

Go through the manuscript chapter by chapter. For each chapter ask: Does it open with something that earns the reader's attention? Is the chapter's one job clear? Does it end with enough momentum to carry to the next?

The Chapter Tension Test

Every chapter needs tension — something unresolved that pulls the reader forward. In fiction: dramatic tension. In non-fiction: intellectual tension — a question not yet fully answered. If a chapter has no tension, readers stop.

Pass 3: Line-Level Revision

Line-Level Checklist

Cut every word that doesn't earn its place. Replace vague words with specific ones. Vary sentence length deliberately. Check for repetition. Read dialogue out loud. Look for passive voice. Give extra attention to the first and last sentences of every chapter.

Assignments

Your Work

Complete each assignment before moving to the next module.

5.1

The Full Read-Through

Read your entire manuscript without making changes. Note: (1) the three things working best, (2) the three biggest structural problems, (3) anything that surprised you.

5.2

Structural Diagnosis

Which chapters need to move? Which need to be cut? What is missing that the book needs? List your structural changes before making them.

5.3

Your Weakest Chapter

Identify the chapter that needs the most work. Describe what is wrong with it and write a plan for how to fix it. Then rewrite it.

5.4

Your Opening and Ending

Rewrite your first paragraph and last paragraph. Write at least three versions of each and choose the strongest.

5.5

Voice Check

Copy one paragraph from the beginning, middle, and end of your manuscript. Read them together. Does your voice sound like the same person throughout?

5.6

The Cut List

List every section, scene, or chapter you know should be cut but haven't yet. Name them here. Then cut them. (Save them in a separate 'cut material' document.)
